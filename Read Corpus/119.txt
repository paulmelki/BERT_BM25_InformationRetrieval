an allocution or allocutus is formal statement made to the court by the defendant who has been found guilty prior to being sentenced it is part of the criminal procedure in some jurisdictions using common law concept an allocution allows the defendant to explain why the sentence should be lenient in plea bargains an allocution may be required of the defendant the defendant explicitly admits specifically and in detail the actions and their reasons in exchange for reduced sentence in principle that removes any doubt as to the exact nature of the defendant guilt in the matter the term allocution is used generally only in jurisdictions in the united states but there are vaguely similar processes in other common law countries in many other jurisdictions it is for the defense lawyer to mitigate on his client behalf and the defendant rarely has the opportunity to speak the right of victims to speak at sentencing is also sometimes referred to as allocution australia in australia the term allocutus is used by the clerk of arraigns or another formal associate of the court it is generally phrased as prisoner at the bar you have been found guilty by jury of your peers of the offense of xyz do you have anything to say as to why the sentence of this court should not now be passed upon you the defense counsel will then make plea in mitigation also called submissions on penalty in an attempt to mitigate the relative seriousness of the offense and heavily refer to and rely upon the defendant previous good character and good works if any the right to make plea in mitigation is absolute if judge or magistrate refuses to hear such plea or does not properly consider it the sentence can be overturned on appeal united states in most of the united states defendants are allowed the opportunity to allocute before sentence is passed some jurisdictions hold that as an absolute right in its absence sentence but not the conviction may be overturned resulting in the need for new sentencing hearing in the federal system federal rules of criminal procedure provides that the court must address the defendant personally in order to permit the defendant to speak or
